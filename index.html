Who I Am

A Writer Shaped by Structure & Struggle

Before ENC 1101, writing was something I did for school a means to an end. My early literacy experiences, from the rigid structure of AICE coursework to the functional flexibility of engineering notebooks, formed the foundation of how I approach writing today.

AICE made me technically precise but emotionally distant from writing. Engineering notebooks gave me permission to write messily and purposefully. Together, these shaped a writing identity grounded in clarity, purpose, and adaptability rather than personal expression alone.

This portfolio collects the work from that semester and reflects on what it taught me about writing, about context, and about how literacy is shaped by the institutions and communities we move through.

"Values of clarity, efficiency, and purpose were planted through coursework related to engineering, and I started to emotionally connect to my writing as my passion for engineering grew."
Owen Smith, Major Assignment 1
"I now see myself as a writer who approaches writing as a tool rather than an art form. I believe this identity is not a limitation or necessarily negative, but a reflection of my literacy history."
Owen Smith, Major Assignment 1 (Revised)

Major Assignment 1

Literacy Autobiography: Writing, Sponsors, and Identity

This essay traces my literacy history through two pivotal experiences: my junior year AICE writing class, which made me technically proficient but emotionally distant from writing, and my engineering notebooks, which taught me that writing could be functional, messy, and personally meaningful.

Using Brandt's concept of literacy sponsors, Rumsey's heritage literacy framework, and Wardle's ideas about context-dependent transfer, I argue that my writing identity pragmatic, clarity-focused, and tool-oriented was not accidental but shaped by layered institutional influences.

Excerpt from Major Assignment 1 (Revised)

Before I signed up for this class, I thought of writing as something I only did for school. It was a way to get grades and complete assignments. I did not think of it as a way to express my thoughts and feelings. Writing was like a job. I did what I had to do.

One of the most influential literacy experiences in my life was my junior year AICE writing class. This course acted as a powerful literacy sponsor, as Brandt would describe it. Brandt defines literacy sponsors as "any agents, local or distant, concrete or abstract, who enable, support, teach, model, as well as recruit, regulate, suppress, or withhold literacy" (Brandt 166). My AICE class both enabled and constrained my literacy development. On the one hand, it significantly improved my technical writing skills. On the other hand, it reshaped my emotional relationship with writing in ways that were limiting.

The course emphasized a rigid structure, strict tone, and near perfect grammar. Essays followed predetermined templates, and deviation often resulted in point deductions or failing grades. Writing became a process of fitting ideas into predefined molds rather than developing them organically.

My time writing engineering notebooks was quite different. When I was in engineering classes, the teachers did not care much about grammar or the quality of the writing. They cared about whether my ideas made sense, whether I wrote down what I did, and whether my logic was sound. Unlike the rigid expectations of AICE writing, engineering notebooks allowed flexibility and imperfection. This aligns closely with Rumsey's concept of heritage literacy, which emphasizes how individuals adapt literacy tools to fit their lived experiences and needs.

Although AICE made me a stronger technical writer, it also left me viewing writing as something restrictive and performative. That frustration shaped how I approached later literacy experiences. By the time I began writing engineering notebooks, I was already looking for a form of writing that felt more practical and connected to the way I actually think.

Today, my feelings about writing are shaped by the difference between my two experiences. I still think it is important to be clear, get to the point, and have a reason for writing. Now I understand how the situation affects my writing, and I am more willing to try different ways of writing.

Major Assignment 2

Workout Plans as a Genre in the Fitness Community

This essay applies genre theory to an artifact from my own life: the workout plans I write and use while training. Drawing on Dirk, Gee, and Jacobson et al., I argue that workout plans are a genre they follow recognizable conventions (sets, reps, rest periods, progressive structure) that reflect the values and practices of the fitness discourse community.

The essay moves from theory to personal artifact, analyzing how my own split-based training plan both conforms to genre conventions and reflects my personal philosophy about fatigue management and muscular recruitment.

Excerpt from Major Assignment 2 (Revised)

Reading and writing are often associated primarily with academic environments such as classrooms, essays, and textbooks. However, literacy practices extend far beyond school and occur constantly in everyday social contexts. Kerry Dirk explains that genres emerge because writers repeatedly encounter similar rhetorical situations, which leads them to develop familiar responses to those situations (Dirk 252).

Workout plans function as a genre because they follow recognizable conventions such as structured organization, measurable training parameters, and progress tracking. These conventions shape how training information is communicated and determine whether a plan appears credible and useful within the fitness community.

The language used in my workout plan directly reflects what Gee describes as an "identity kit." Terms such as "progressive overload," "volume," "compound lifts," and "fatigue management" are not simply technical vocabulary; they signal participation in the shared values and practices of the fitness discourse community. For example, when I organize my plan around upper and lower splits, track total weekly training volume, and structure rest days to manage recovery, I am using the same language and decision making framework that experienced lifters use to evaluate effective programming.

The structure of my workout plan reflects a lot of the conventions found in programs online, but it is also shaped directly by how I train and what I have learned works best for me. I follow a structure built around repeating upper and lower sessions, organized as four days on, one day off. My upper days are longer, around seventeen sets, because of the number of muscle groups involved, while my lower days are closer to eight sets.

Instead of doing multiple sets of the same exercise, I spread my volume across different movements that train the same muscle in slightly different ways. For example, rather than repeating pec flies, I will do one set of a standard fly and one set of a low-to-high cable fly to target different functions of the chest. This approach comes from my own experience prioritizing more complete muscular recruitment rather than simply repeating the same movement.

Ultimately, examining workout plans as a genre reveals how writing functions as a socially shaped activity. The conventions of the genre influence how the text is written, how it is interpreted by others, and how it affects the behavior of the people who use it. What may initially seem like a personal planning document is actually part of a broader system of communication within the fitness community.

Excerpt from Major Assignment 3

Dear Professor Gonzalez,

I am writing this letter in response to the feedback I received on Major Assignments 1 and 2 throughout the semester. The comments from both my peers and my instructor have enabled me to identify the strengths and weaknesses in my drafts and to make more purposeful and meaningful revisions in my writing. As Jillian Grauman explains, interpreting and acting on feedback is a learned skill that develops through practice and reflection (Grauman). I now recognize that feedback is not simply a list of corrections but a chance to engage more deeply with my writing and its effect on readers.

The most significant feedback I received on Major Assignment 1 addressed my use of MLA citations and integration of sources. My instructor observed that, although I referenced course readings such as Brandt and Rumsey, my citations were inconsistent and sometimes improperly formatted. Peers also noted that some transitions between literacy events were abrupt, which made it difficult to follow the connection between my AICE experience and my engineering notebook experience.

In response, I concentrated my revisions on three key areas. First, I systematically reviewed all in-text citations and the Works Cited page to correct formatting errors and ensure alignment with MLA 9th edition guidelines. Second, I revised the transitional sentences between my two literary events to clarify the thematic connections. Third, I rewrote my thesis to more clearly articulate the argument that my literacy experiences resulted in a writing identity grounded in purposeful clarity rather than expressive freedom.

For Major Assignment 2, I revisited every source I cited and reconstructed the in-text citations and Works Cited entries to ensure proper MLA formatting. I also deepened my analysis by revisiting Gee's concept of identity kits and explicitly applying it to the vocabulary in my workout plan adding a paragraph that examines how terms such as "progressive overload" and "one rep maximum" function as both lexis and indicators of membership within the fitness discourse community.

Reviewing both assignments through the perspective of Taczak and Robertson's work on metacognition has been particularly insightful. They argue that reflective writing enables students to move beyond task completion and toward a more profound understanding of their own writing processes. This letter has served as a form of self-aware reflection for me. Rather than viewing revision as a chore or a reaction to criticism, I now see it as an integral part of the writing process.

Respectfully,
Owen Smith

E-Portfolio Reflection

Looking Back

The portfolio is organized around the six course learning outcomes each one connected to specific artifacts that demonstrate engagement with that outcome. The goal was to select work that showed not just what was produced, but how thinking developed across the semester.

Outcome One pushed me to reflect on my writing history in a way I'd never done before. The Literacy Autobiography required looking backward tracing where my habits came from and why they felt so fixed. The Genre Analysis required something different: taking a familiar, everyday practice and examining it as though it were new. Both were uncomfortable in different ways, and both produced better thinking because of that discomfort.

Outcomes Two and Three sharpened my awareness of how much context shapes communication. Reading Journal 4 helped me recognize that I adjust my writing style based on community expectations almost automatically a shift I'd never consciously noticed before. The Visual Text Analysis extended that into a new mode entirely, requiring me to apply analytical frameworks to something that wasn't language at all.

For Outcome Three, applying Dirk's genre framework to workout plans was the turning point. What I'd assumed were just practical conventions sets, reps, rest periods, split structure turned out to carry rhetorical weight. They signal membership and credibility within the fitness community. That realization reframed the entire essay.

Outcome Four is demonstrated most clearly in the distance between my Genre Analysis draft and the final version. The draft had the core argument but didn't develop it far enough. Getting from one to the other meant making deliberate choices: what the essay needed more of, what was taking up space without earning it. That is a different kind of writing than simply adding content.

Outcome Five required examining how institutional contexts shape what writing is allowed to look like and who benefits from those standards. The AICE program improved my technical precision while narrowing what I thought good writing could be. That tension runs through the Literacy Autobiography and connects directly to questions of access and power in literacy education.

Outcome Six is where everything came together. Revision, this semester, stopped being a cleanup step and became something more like rethinking. The final Genre Analysis is a structurally different argument from the draft shaped by feedback, reader perspective, and a clearer understanding of what the essay was actually trying to do.

Semester Growth

Before & After

Understanding of Genre

My thinking about genre changed fundamentally this semester from "type of writing" to "socially shaped response to recurring situations."

BeforeGenre = category label (essay, email, report)
AfterGenre = tool that enables action within a discourse community (Dirk)

Relationship with Feedback

I moved from treating feedback as judgment to treating it as structural information a map of where my reader got lost.

BeforeFeedback = criticism to defend against
AfterFeedback = reader experience data that drives revision (Grauman)

View of Literacy

I used to think literacy was about grammar and grades. Now I understand it as a set of socially situated practices shaped by sponsors, institutions, and power.

BeforeLiteracy = correct grammar + academic formatting
AfterLiteracy = context-dependent, sponsor-shaped, power-inflected practice (Brandt)

Writing Identity

I entered this class thinking my tool-oriented relationship with writing was a limitation. I leave understanding it as a legitimate and conscious identity.

Before"I'm not a real writer because I don't enjoy writing for its own sake."
After"I write purposefully and adaptively that is its own kind of literacy strength."
